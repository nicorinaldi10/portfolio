What is a CranioBot?

A Craniobot is a surgical robot designed to perform craniotomies on small rodents, automating a process that is traditionally manual, slow, and highly dependent on the surgeon's skill. This project integrates computer vision and neural networks to enhance precision by predicting physical coordinates from images. Combined with an automated CNC system, this technology improves positioning accuracy and enhances research efficiency in neuroscience by making brain access procedures safer, faster, and more reliable.




Key Components of the System

Genmitsu 3018-PROVer CNC Machine:

The Genmitsu 3018-PROVer CNC Machine is a compact and precise tool that forms the foundation of the system. Originally designed for cutting materials like wood and plastics, it has been adapted to perform accurate skull cutting in rodents. It operates with three stepper motors that control movement along different axes and includes a safety emergency button to ensure secure operation.


Thorlabs DCC1645C Camera:

The Thorlabs DCC1645C Camera is a high-precision imaging device used for real-time monitoring. Its integration into the system allows for accurate positioning and visualization, making the process more efficient and reliable.


Camera Lens:

The Camera Lens enhances image clarity and lighting control, ensuring proper focus for capturing precise details during the procedure.

Together, these components enable a fully automated and accurate system, improving both the efficiency and safety of the process.




Calibration and Model Training Process:

The calibration process begins when the Start command is activated, ensuring no previous calibration is running. The camera captures an initial reference image of the calibration block before any modifications, saving it as before_intervention.png.

The system then executes a predefined sequence to drill calibration holes. After this step, a new image is taken, saved as after_intervention.png, and compared to the initial one. The algorithm identifies differences, detects circular contours, and extracts their coordinates.



Once the calibration data is collected, it is processed to train a neural network that maps pixel coordinates from the camera to real-world physical coordinates in the CNC machine workspace. This ensures precise positioning and accurate tool movement.

The trained model enables automated positioning, improving precision in the procedure. Adjustments are made to compensate for any discrepancies between calibration and testing setups, ensuring a fully integrated and automated calibration system that enhances accuracy and efficiency in the surgical process.


Trajectory display and execution

The program allows to preview the trajectory both in 3D and projected on the figure with a range of green and red colors, indicating the highest and lowest points in the Z dimension respectively. In addition, the terminal displays the list of Z coordinates in their corresponding color, which it checks against the projection on the frame.




The tests for 3D trajectories were conducted directly on test bricks with skulls placed on a rectangular prism. These tests focused on evaluating the system's ability to perform helical and layered movements, verifying the CNC machine’s coordination of the X, Y, and Z axes.

The execution of these movements was carried out using the G01 function of the G-Code, ensuring precise and controlled linear movements. During testing, deviations between predicted and actual positions were analyzed, identifying areas for improvement in vertical displacements and axis synchronization.

As part of the refinement process, the feed and depth parameters were calibrated to ensure smooth and uniform movement along the Z-axis. These adjustments improved the system’s ability to replicate three-dimensional paths accurately.

The results demonstrated that the system could reproduce 3D trajectories with accuracy comparable to 2D movements, confirming its viability for more complex applications.



Template Alignment

The Lambda, Bregma, and Align Template buttons help manage key anatomical reference points and template positioning within the workspace. Since mouse skulls have minimal variability, identifying the Lambda and Bregma points allows for precise template placement, ensuring accurate targeting of brain regions.

When the user selects Lambda or Bregma, they simply click the respective button and mark the location on the image. The program captures and stores these points, allowing for template alignment. The Align Template function then adjusts the position, rotation, and scale of the template to match these reference points, ensuring that planned trajectories align perfectly with the actual work area.

To further refine the alignment, position and size sliders allow the user to make fine adjustments. Position sliders move the template along the X and Y axes, while sizing sliders adjust its scale uniformly to maintain correct proportions. At any moment, all marks can be cleared and reset using the Reset Template button, ensuring flexibility in the calibration process.

This system enhances precision and reproducibility, making it crucial for applications where accuracy is essential, such as surgical navigation and 3D modeling.
